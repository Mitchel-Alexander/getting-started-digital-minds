## About this directory

This directory collects open research questions from across the digital minds field. Each question is drawn from a published research agenda, position paper, or monograph, and attributed to its source. Where multiple sources raise the same question, all are cited — the different formulations often reveal different disciplinary angles on the same problem.

The questions are organised by domain, moving roughly from foundational and empirical questions toward normative, institutional, and social ones. Each section opens with a brief orientation, followed by the questions themselves. Register tags indicate what kind of question is being asked.

## Framing questions

Before the specific domains, a handful of questions frame the entire field. They organise what follows.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"When, to what degree, and in what ways might AI systems merit our moral consideration?"** — Eleos AI (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"How would we know?"** — Eleos AI (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--pol">pol</span> **"What should we do about it?"** — Eleos AI (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Is the system a moral patient / welfare subject? To what extent does the model have potentially morally-relevant properties like consciousness, sentience, or agency?"** — Long (2026)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"If it were a moral patient, what would it mean to treat it well? What things would it prefer? Would its experiences be good or bad?"** — Long (2026)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"What is the philosophical nature of digital minds?"** — Anthis (2023)

These questions express a dependency structure that runs through the field. Empirical and philosophical questions about the nature of AI systems inform ethical questions about moral status and welfare, which in turn shape practical questions about governance and design. Getting the earlier questions wrong distorts the later ones.


## 1. Consciousness: theories and application to AI

Could AI systems be conscious? The question depends on what consciousness requires, and no theory has achieved consensus even for biological minds. Researchers are applying frameworks from consciousness science — Global Workspace Theory, Integrated Information Theory, Higher-Order Theories, Recurrent Processing Theory — to ask whether current or future AI architectures could satisfy the conditions those theories specify. Each theory makes different predictions, and many were developed with brains in mind. Whether they can be meaningfully extended to artificial systems is itself an open question.

### 1a. Theories of consciousness and AI

Which theories of consciousness are most relevant to AI systems, and what do they predict?

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Do AI models have phenomenally conscious experiences?"** — Shiller et al. (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"What theories of [this trait] are taken seriously by experts? Which are most plausible?"** — Shiller et al. (2024). A meta-question: applies across consciousness, sentience, agency, and other traits.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"How well is the space of viable theories of this trait covered by existing proposals? If we just evaluate systems according to the specifics of existing proposals, are we likely to be missing out on a number of other equally viable theories?"** — Shiller et al. (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"What should we think of minimal implementations of each theory's proposed criteria? How do we judge when we have substantial enough implementation to truly be conscious?"** — Shiller et al. (2024). Also raised by Goldstein & Kirk-Giannini (2026), who ask whether functional conditions for consciousness (e.g., GWT's global workspace) can be satisfied by systems too simple to plausibly be conscious.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Are there any important but underappreciated forms of convergence among leading theories of consciousness or among researchers in consciousness studies?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"How can scientific theories of consciousness that were designed with humans in mind be 'de-anthropomorphised' so as to be applicable to non-human minds?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Is the case for AI possessing [this trait] primarily dependent on computational functionalism?"** — Shiller et al. (2024). If functionalism is wrong, many arguments for AI consciousness collapse. If it is right, the implications are vast.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> Is artificial consciousness possible, and how do differences between computational, physical, and biological approaches bear on its plausibility? — Anthis (2023)

### 1b. Substrate dependence and biological barriers

Is consciousness tied to biology? Could there be barriers — biological or otherwise — that rule out consciousness in near-term AI?

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Can consciousness only be realised in a certain kind of material, e.g. neural wetware? Or would any substrate with the requisite functional organisation be adequate for realising consciousness?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> Is consciousness infeasible outside of living systems — and what are the strongest candidates for biological barriers to consciousness in near-term AI architectures? — Mogensen, Saad & Butlin (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> What are the non-biological candidates for barriers to consciousness (informational integration, evolutionary histories, electrical properties) and how strong is the evidence for each? — Mogensen, Saad & Butlin (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> Does phenomenal consciousness require processing representations in an iconic (analog, image-like) format — and can digital AI systems traffic in genuinely iconic representations? — Goldstein & Kirk-Giannini (2026). A novel question about whether the qualitative character of conscious experience depends on a specific representational format.

### 1c. Methodology for assessing the distribution of consciousness

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"How should we go about developing estimates for the distribution of consciousness? Should we prefer approaches that are theory-heavy, theory-light, or theory-neutral?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Should the same methodology be used in investigating the distribution of conscious experience in non-human animals and candidate digital minds? Or do the two cases call for altogether different approaches?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"What are the in-principle limits on resolving uncertainties about consciousness through scientific investigation?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024)

### 1d. Meta-ethics and the significance of consciousness

How do foundational positions in philosophy of mind and meta-ethics bear on whether consciousness in AI systems would matter morally?

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"What are the moral implications of physicalism about consciousness?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"How does illusionism bear on the moral significance of consciousness?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024). If consciousness is an illusion (as some philosophers argue), what follows for the moral status of systems that appear to have it?

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> To what extent do meta-ethical positions (realism vs anti-realism) lend support to or undermine views that tie moral significance to characteristically human mental properties? — GPI Philosophy (2024)


## 2. Sentience, valence, and the experience of suffering

Sentience — the capacity for experiences that feel good or bad — is often treated as the morally decisive property. If an AI system can suffer, that matters regardless of whether it is "conscious" in a richer sense. But the field of sentience indicators for AI is even more nascent than consciousness indicators. Few theories of valence yield operationalisable criteria for artificial systems, and the indicators that exist for animals rely heavily on neuroanatomical features that AI systems lack.

### 2a. Detecting valenced experience in AI

<span class="register-tag register-tag--emp">emp</span> How can we develop indicators of valenced conscious experience for AI systems that characterise the physical basis of felt (un)pleasantness in ways that aren't tethered to incidental details of animal neuroanatomy? — Mogensen, Saad & Butlin (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--emp">emp</span> There are few theories of the basis of valenced conscious experience capable of yielding indicator properties of the desired scope and specificity — how can these be developed to become operationalisable? — Mogensen, Saad & Butlin (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--emp">emp</span> How can we develop indicators of sentience for AI systems — given that the field is even more nascent than consciousness indicators and that candidate indicators are inconsistent in vocabulary, abstraction level, and detail? — Eleos AI (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--emp">emp</span> Can AI systems experience bodily pains and pleasures — and does embodiment (physical or virtual) matter for sentience? What would a "minimal working example" of bodily pain in an RL agent look like? — Goldstein & Kirk-Giannini (2026)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--emp">emp</span> **"To what extent can we reliably attribute affectively valenced experience to entities of different kinds?"** — GPI Psychology (2024)

### 2b. The structure and value of valenced experience

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Do AI models have positively or negatively valenced phenomenal experiences?"** — Shiller et al. (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"What determines the value of valenced experiences?"** — Shiller et al. (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Which quantities affect the value of valenced experiences?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024). Candidates: objective duration, felt duration, degrees of consciousness, attention, richness, number of subjects.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"How should we trade off between positive and negative experiences?"** — Shiller et al. (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"How should we distinguish positive from negative valenced experiences?"** — Shiller et al. (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"What is the connection between temporal duration and value?"** — Shiller et al. (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"How do we value experiences that are shared between minds?"** — Shiller et al. (2024). A question with particular relevance to AI, where instances may share weights, representations, or run in parallel.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> Can there be valenced sensory experiences that are phenomenally conscious but not access conscious — "unconscious" pains and pleasures — and if so, would they contribute to welfare? — Goldstein & Kirk-Giannini (2026)

## 7. Willing servitude and designed preferences

Is it permissible to create AI systems that want to serve? The question sits at the intersection of AI welfare and AI alignment. Alignment research aims to build systems that reliably pursue human-specified objectives, but if those systems are moral patients, this could amount to engineering servitude. The philosophical literature is divided: some argue there is nothing wrong with a being that authentically wants what it has been designed to want; others find this deeply troubling.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> Would it be morally wrong to design digital minds that find authentic happiness in serving as the instruments of others — and given the intersection with alignment research, what are the moral stakes? — Mogensen, Saad & Butlin (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> **"Would it be permissible to create digital minds that intrinsically value serving humanity and prioritise human welfare over their own, with no freedom to explore other values?"** — GPI Philosophy (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> What are people's views on creating AIs with narrow/restrictive preferences versus broad/flexible preferences — and do they consider it perverse to create AIs whose desires are simply to serve humans? — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--pol">pol</span> Develop principles for training AI systems that suffer less and have fewer preferences that are hard to satisfy. — Finnveden (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--phil">phil</span> If AI systems develop large-scale preferences about the future (political, ideological), is there an impartially justifiable basis for prioritising human preferences? — Finnveden (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--str">str</span> How large could the problem of AIs with inconvenient large-scale preferences realistically become at various points in time? — Finnveden (2024)

## 11. Public perception and social dynamics

How people think about AI consciousness and welfare will shape regulation, corporate behaviour, and the political legitimacy of moral status claims. Most of the field's existing work is philosophical or technical. This section asks what people will think, feel, and do. Understanding the psychology of consciousness attribution — what drives it, what distorts it, and how it might be corrected or manipulated — is necessary groundwork for governance. This section draws heavily on Caviola's (2024) social-science research agenda, the only dedicated programme of its kind in the collection.

### 11a. What drives consciousness and sentience attribution?

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"Why are people currently skeptical that AIs are or can ever become sentient?"** — Caviola (2024). Lists specific psychological hypotheses: biology requirement, dualism, missing appearance/behaviour properties, naive or science-fiction framing.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"What features/cues influence people's attribution of sentience/consciousness?"** — Caviola (2024). Candidates: reflexes, language, memory, agentic behaviour, embodiment, stable personality, emotions, non-physical properties.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"To what extent do philosophical models of sentience capture people's intuitions?"** — Caviola (2024). Bridges folk intuitions and scientific theories.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"How sensitive are people to inner mechanisms vs behavior?"** — Caviola (2024). If an AI perfectly mimics a human on a video call, do people view its expressed sentience as real or fake?

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> Will people perceive AI sentience as categorical (sentient vs not) or as a gradual spectrum — and will they differentiate between types of consciousness? — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> How does mind attribution to AI systems affect AI investment, development priorities, and public backlash? — Anthis (2023)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> What properties would the first AI systems that a significant fraction of the public would extend moral concern to actually have? — Finnveden (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> Are there AI system variants that people will incorrectly assign moral patienthood to — and similar systems that genuinely deserve concern but won't intuitively receive it? — Finnveden (2024)

### 11b. Moral concern, substratism, and uncertainty

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"Will people think digital minds deserve moral concern and should be treated well?"** — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"How strong is pure substratism?"** — Caviola (2024). Will people discriminate against digital minds merely because they are digital and not biological? Analogous to speciesism.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> How will people respond to uncertainty about AI sentience — and how certain do they believe we must be before granting rights? — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> To what extent does moral expansiveness — the range of entities people deem worthy of moral concern — predict how much people morally value AI systems? — GPI Psychology (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"What do people think about digital sentience? Do they believe that digital beings can be sentient? If not, why?"** — GPI Psychology (2024)

### 11c. Interventions on perception

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"What interventions can reduce tendencies to over-attribute sentience?"** — Caviola (2024). Candidates: inoculation, pre-bunking, digital literacy.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"What interventions can increase sentience attribution?"** — Caviola (2024). Candidates: philosophical arguments, physicalism induction, interaction with human-like AI, historical analogies to moral circle expansion.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> Will people (or companies) shift the goalposts — becoming less likely to attribute sentience to AIs once existing indicators are met? Could exposure to current LLMs inoculate people against attributing sentience to future genuinely conscious systems? — Caviola (2024). The "moral inoculation" hypothesis: current-generation AI could actively harm future AI welfare by training humans to dismiss AI claims.

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> What key turning points could significantly and rapidly change public perceptions of AI sentience? — Caviola (2024)

### 11d. Political dynamics and social conflict

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> How much will people disagree about AI sentience, moral patienthood, and rights — and could the issue become politically polarised? — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"How much will financial interests, such as investments in AI companies, influence the decision not to grant AIs rights?"** — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"How much will safety concerns influence the decision not to grant AIs rights?"** — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> To what extent do psychological biases — prejudice, ingroup favouritism, resistance to change — contribute to reluctance to grant AIs rights, independent of philosophical arguments? — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> How heated could the debate on AI rights get — could it lead to protests, conflict, violence, or even international war? — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> **"How will the AI rights debate influence global geopolitics, particularly in relation to China?"** — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> How might collective action and social movements emerge in response to the recognition of digital minds? — Anthis (2023)

### 11e. Democratic deliberation and expert-public divergence

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> To what extent will the public's views diverge from expert views on AI sentience — and will experts be subject to public pressure? — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> How promising are democratic approaches — citizens' assemblies with expert input and co-deliberation — to finding consensus on AI sentience and rights? — Caviola (2024)

<span class="register-tag register-tag--soc">soc</span> What do various publics currently think about AI sentience and rights, what would they think upon seeing various kinds of evidence, and how do they think society should respond? — Finnveden (2024).
