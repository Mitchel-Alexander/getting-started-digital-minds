Digital minds is a small but growing field and currently lacks some of the formalised progression pathways that you might find in more developed areas like AI safety and governance. Individual contributions carry real weight, though. The community is small enough that a thoughtful blog post or a well-organised reading group can build recognition quickly. Dedicated research positions are starting to appear at AI labs, non-profits, and university research centres, but for now the field rewards people who can independently identify and pursue useful work. Early entrants who are comfortable working within persistent uncertainty have a genuine chance to help define the norms, research agendas, and institutional practices of a field whose shape is not yet settled.

The questions the field asks span philosophy, cognitive science, AI research, law, and governance. No single discipline can answer them, which is why the field needs people from many backgrounds and why entering from an adjacent area is a legitimate path.

This matters for how you think about entering. A direct path runs through dedicated digital minds programmes and organisations, outlined below. An indirect path runs through adjacent disciplines, bringing relevant expertise into spaces where these questions are not yet being asked. The indirect path carries more uncertainty, but many of the field's most pressing needs — connecting research to governance, bridging disciplinary divides — are best addressed by people with deep roots elsewhere.

Below, we first discuss adjacent pathways and then the emerging direct pipeline. We also outline two broad disciplinary tracks, technical research and governance, with guidance by career stage.


## Technical Track

Research on consciousness, cognition, welfare assessment, and interpretability as applied to AI systems. This track draws on philosophy, neuroscience, computer science, and cognitive science. The work is inherently interdisciplinary, and the most productive researchers in this space tend to be those who can read across at least two of these literatures.

### Students

The most useful thing a student can do is build strong foundations in one contributing discipline while developing literacy in at least one other.

**Philosophy and cognitive science students** should take courses in philosophy of mind, consciousness studies, and ethics. Familiarity with the major consciousness theories (GWT, IIT, Higher-Order Theories) is essential. Courses in AI, machine learning, or computational neuroscience are valuable for building cross-disciplinary fluency.

**Computer science students** should look for courses in AI safety, interpretability, and machine learning. Philosophy of mind and ethics courses provide the conceptual frameworks that technical work in this field requires. Engaging with interpretability research (mechanistic interpretability, representation engineering) provides skills that are directly transferable.

**Neuroscience students** should focus on consciousness science, computational neuroscience, and cognitive modelling. The relationship between biological and artificial minds is one of the field's most productive areas of inquiry, and one where the ability to communicate findings across disciplinary boundaries is especially valuable.

**Useful actions:**
- Form or join a reading group on digital minds, AI consciousness, or AI welfare
- Write a thesis or dissertation on a digital minds topic (supervisors may be open even if they do not specialise in this area)
- Attend online events and field-building conferences such as [Sentient Futures](https://www.sentientfutures.ai/) or [EA Global](https://www.eaglobal.org/)
- Apply for a mentorship or fellowship programme (see [Digital Minds Programmes](#digital-minds-programmes) below)
- Follow the Digital Minds Newsletter and the research outputs of groups like Eleos AI and Rethink Priorities

### Early Career

Most early-career researchers enter digital minds through a PhD in a contributing discipline. Dedicated digital minds PhD positions are rare but growing. More commonly, researchers pursue doctoral work in philosophy of mind, consciousness science, cognitive science, AI safety, or interpretability, and orient their research toward digital minds questions.

**Where to look:**
- PhD programmes with supervisors working on consciousness and AI (Cambridge, NYU, ANU, Sussex, among others)
- Research positions at dedicated organisations (Eleos AI, Rethink Priorities, PRISM)
- Interpretability and welfare research roles at AI labs (Anthropic, Google DeepMind)
- Postdoctoral fellowships in consciousness science, philosophy of mind, or AI ethics
- [Career Development and Transition Funding](https://coefficientgiving.org/funds/global-catastrophic-risks-opportunities/career-development-and-transition-funding/) from Coefficient Giving, for researchers moving into areas relevant to global catastrophic risks

**Useful actions:**
- Publish in relevant venues (journals like *Neuroscience of Consciousness*, *Minds and Machines*, *Philosophy & Technology*; conferences like ASSC, NeurIPS workshops)
- Present work at digital minds workshops and seminars
- Build relationships with researchers across disciplines. The field is small enough that direct outreach is welcomed.

### Mid-Career

Researchers with established expertise in adjacent fields are among the most valuable potential contributors. Consciousness scientists, interpretability researchers, animal cognition researchers, and ethicists all bring skills that transfer directly. What they also bring, and what the field needs just as much, is the ability to see digital minds questions from outside the assumptions that have shaped the field so far.

**Routes in:**
- Apply existing research methods to digital minds questions (e.g., extend interpretability techniques to welfare-relevant model properties, or apply consciousness metrics developed for clinical settings to AI systems)
- Collaborate with researchers already in the field. Co-authored work is a natural entry point.
- Seek visiting positions or sabbaticals at centres working on digital minds (Cambridge CDM, NYU)
- Engage with funding calls that support cross-disciplinary work on AI and consciousness


## Governance Track

Policy, law, rights, institutional design, and governance under uncertainty as applied to digital minds. This track draws on policy studies, law, ethics, political philosophy, and social science.

### Students

Digital minds governance is a niche within a niche. Few programmes teach it directly. The most effective preparation is to build strong foundations in a relevant governance discipline while engaging with digital minds questions through independent study.

**Policy and law students** should take courses in technology governance, AI policy, and international law. Courses in ethics and philosophy of mind provide the substantive grounding this work requires. Familiarity with animal welfare policy is particularly relevant, given the parallels in extending moral consideration under uncertainty.

**Philosophy and social science students** should engage with the governance and policy dimensions of their work. Social scientists bring essential methods for studying public attitudes toward AI consciousness, expert disagreement, institutional responses to moral uncertainty, and the political dynamics of extending moral consideration. Understanding how knowledge claims about consciousness or moral status interact with institutional decision-making is central to this track. So is the ability to communicate findings to policymakers and publics who may encounter these ideas for the first time.

**Useful actions:**
- Write policy briefs or seminar papers on digital minds topics
- Engage with AI governance groups and student policy organisations
- Seek internships at organisations working on AI governance, animal welfare policy, or technology ethics
- Attend events hosted by Cambridge Digital Minds, PRISM, or similar initiatives

### Early Career

Early-career governance professionals typically enter digital minds from AI governance, animal welfare policy, technology ethics, or legal scholarship. Dedicated digital minds governance roles are scarce. Most people in this space hold broader positions (AI policy researcher, technology ethics fellow) and orient part of their work toward digital minds questions.

**Where to look:**
- Policy research roles at think tanks working on AI governance (e.g., Centre for the Governance of AI, CSER, Ada Lovelace Institute)
- Legal research positions on AI rights or personhood
- Roles at organisations with explicit digital minds programmes (PRISM, Rethink Priorities)
- Fellowships in AI governance, technology policy, or responsible innovation

**Useful actions:**
- Publish policy analysis or legal commentary on digital minds governance questions
- Contribute to consultations and policy processes on AI governance, flagging digital minds dimensions
- Build expertise in an adjacent governance area (AI safety governance, animal welfare regulation) and connect it to digital minds

### Mid-Career

Experienced governance professionals from AI policy, animal welfare, bioethics, or international law bring institutional knowledge, credibility, and networks that the field urgently needs. They also bring something harder to teach: an understanding of how new ideas gain traction in institutional settings, and how to make a case for taking seriously a possibility that most decision-makers have not yet considered.

**Routes in:**
- Bring digital minds questions into existing governance mandates (if you work on AI policy, animal welfare, or technology regulation, these questions are increasingly within scope)
- Advise or collaborate with digital minds research organisations
- Participate in expert convenings on AI consciousness, moral status, or welfare governance
- Pursue visiting fellowships at centres working on digital minds governance


## Adjacent Pathways

Digital minds is perhaps best understood, at least for now, as a cause area positioned between multiple convergent disciplines rather than a coherent discipline in its own right. This creates an unusual situation for someone thinking about how to enter.

The most legible route is to join a dedicated digital minds programme or organisation, and we outline that pipeline further below. But the field also needs people embedded in adjacent institutions who bring digital minds questions into spaces where they are not yet being asked. An AI policy researcher who raises welfare considerations in regulatory discussions. An interpretability researcher who asks consciousness-relevant questions about model internals. A journalist who makes these questions legible for public audiences. In a field this young, these contributions may be where much of the impact lies.

Adjacent paths carry more uncertainty about whether you end up doing sustained digital minds work. What these roles offer in return is an outside perspective: familiarity with how another field handles similar problems, and the ability to identify what digital minds has yet to learn from it. A willingness to recognise the limits of one's own discipline and to take seriously what others have to offer is itself a form of expertise the field needs.

Below are some adjacent areas where digital minds questions are increasingly relevant, with examples of programmes and roles that can serve as entry points.

**AI governance and policy.** Fellowships and research positions at governance organisations build skills in policy analysis, institutional design, and stakeholder engagement, all of which transfer directly to digital minds governance. Programmes such as [GovAI](https://www.governance.ai/) research fellowships, [ERA (Existential Risk Alliance)](https://www.existentialriskalliance.org/) fellowships, and policy roles at organisations like the [Ada Lovelace Institute](https://www.adalovelaceinstitute.org/) or [CSER](https://www.cser.ac.uk/) offer structured entry into AI governance. From these positions, advocating for the inclusion of digital minds considerations in policy frameworks is a concrete and increasingly welcomed contribution.

**AI ethics and responsible AI.** Roles in AI ethics, responsible innovation, and technology assessment sit close to digital minds questions without always recognising the connection. Existing work on fairness, accountability, and the social impacts of AI stops short of asking whether the systems themselves might matter morally. Researchers and practitioners in these roles are well placed to extend that boundary.

**Technical AI research.** Mechanistic interpretability, model evaluations, representation engineering, and AI safety research all develop methods that bear directly on digital minds questions. Researchers working on what AI systems are doing internally are building the toolkit that consciousness and welfare assessment will eventually draw on. Programmes like [MATS](https://www.matsprogram.org/) and [SPAR AI](https://sparai.org/) provide structured entry into this space.

**Journalism and public communication.** How digital minds questions are framed for public audiences shapes policy and public attitudes. Journalism fellowships focused on AI and technology, such as the [Tarbell Fellowship](https://tarbellfellowship.org/), provide training in science communication and investigative reporting that can be directed toward digital minds topics. The ability to communicate complex and weighty ideas to non-specialist audiences is a skill the field urgently needs. These questions will eventually become matters of public concern, and the quality of that public conversation depends on the people who frame it.

**Philosophy, neuroscience, and cognitive science.** Doctoral and postdoctoral work in philosophy of mind, consciousness science, or cognitive science remains one of the most natural adjacent paths. Researchers pursuing questions about consciousness, subjective experience, or mental representation in biological systems are building expertise that transfers directly. The step into digital minds often involves asking whether the same questions apply to artificial systems. Increasingly, supervisors and departments are open to this.


## Digital Minds Programmes

A growing number of programmes now address digital minds questions directly. What follows represents the current practitioner community's best attempt to formalise a pathway from newcomer to active contributor. It is almost certainly only one route among many, and the people building it would be the first to say they are uncertain it is the best one. The field is too young and the questions too unsettled for anyone to claim a proven formula. What this pipeline does offer is structure, and for those who want to specialise from the start, that matters.


### Orientation

Start here. These resources assume no prior knowledge and provide a working understanding of what digital minds research is, why it matters, and what the open questions look like.

- Bradford Saad and Andreas Mogensen, [Digital Minds I: Issues in the Philosophy of Mind and Cognitive Science](https://philpapers.org/archive/SAADMI-2.pdf) (2026). A purpose-built introduction to the philosophical and cognitive science questions at the heart of digital minds research. Covers consciousness, propositional attitudes, personal identity, and methodology.
- Andreas Mogensen and Bradford Saad, [Digital Minds II: Ethical Issues](https://philpapers.org/archive/MOGDMI.pdf) (2026). The companion paper, covering moral standing, welfare, rights, decision-making under uncertainty, and practical measures. Together these two papers offer the most comprehensive academic introduction to the field available.
- 80,000 Hours, [Moral Status of Digital Minds](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/moral-status-digital-minds/). A clear, accessible overview of why this field matters and what is at stake.
- Avi Parrack, [Digital Minds: A Quickstart Guide](https://aviparrack.substack.com/p/digital-minds-a-quickstart-guide). A curated reading list for readers ranging from curious beginners to aspiring contributors.
- [The Digital Minds Newsletter](https://www.digitalminds.news/). A regular publication tracking developments across the field.


### Structured Learning and Mentorship

Once oriented, the next step is guided engagement. Working on a supervised project, joining a cohort, or completing a structured course. Several programmes now offer this, spanning technical research, governance, and ethics. Many cross disciplinary boundaries, which is itself instructive: the experience of working alongside people trained in different traditions is part of what these programmes teach.

- [Cambridge Digital Minds Online Course](https://digitalminds.cam/course/). An eight-week course covering consciousness theories, methods for evaluating AI mental states, public perception, AI safety connections, and governance. Developed with specialists from Cambridge, NYU, Oxford, and other institutions. Free, approximately 3.5 hours per week. Currently in pre-launch, with expressions of interest open.
- [Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars Program](https://neuromatch.io/ai-sentience-scholars/). A six-month, part-time, remote mentored research programme for early-career researchers (Master's level to early postdoc). Projects range from testing consciousness theories in large language models to computational models of personhood and moral status. Includes workshops, seminars, publication opportunities, and a stipend.
- [Future Impact Group Fellowship](https://futureimpact.group/fellowship). A twelve-week, part-time, remote research fellowship with three tracks: AI Policy, Philosophy for Safe AI, and AI Sentience. The AI Sentience track focuses on artificial agent welfare, consciousness models, and governance implications. Eight or more hours per week, with mentored project work and career guidance.
- [PIVOTAL Research](https://www.pivotal-research.org/mentors). A mentorship programme that frequently includes mentors working on AI governance and policy relevant to digital minds. Quarterly cohorts with searchable mentor directories.
- [Sentient Futures Fellowship](https://www.sentientfutures.ai/). A programme exploring intersections of AI, animal welfare, and technology, with learner and facilitator tracks. Relevant for those interested in the welfare dimensions of digital minds and cross-species moral consideration.


### Intensive Development

For those ready to commit more deeply, a smaller number of programmes offer immersive, residential, or full-time engagement with digital minds research.

- [Cambridge Digital Minds Fellowship](https://digitalminds.cam/fellowship/). A five-day residential programme at Jesus College, Cambridge, followed by the two-day Digital Minds Strategy Workshop. Fifteen fellows are paired with mentors and work through modules on technical foundations, societal strategy, and project development. Fully funded with travel, accommodation, and stipend. Applicants are expected to complete the CDM online course beforehand. Run in partnership with Rethink Priorities and PRISM.
- [Longview Career Transition Fellowships on Digital Sentience](https://www.longview.org/digital-sentience-consortium/career-transition-fellowships-on-digital-sentience/). Fellowships designed for researchers transitioning into digital sentience work from adjacent fields. Currently closed, but indicative of the growing infrastructure supporting career movement into this area.


### Contributing to the Field

Progression from learning to contributing is not always a clean step. Many people begin contributing while still in structured programmes. The following are venues and opportunities where emerging researchers can present work, build networks, and help shape where the field goes next.

- **Cambridge Digital Minds Workshop.** A research-focused convening attached to the CDM Fellowship, featuring structured outputs and engagement with senior researchers.
- **Sentient Futures conferences.** Events held in the Bay Area, New York, and London, bringing together researchers and practitioners working on sentience, welfare, and technology. Talks and panels are [available on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/@sentfutures/videos).
- **Publishing.** Relevant venues include *Neuroscience of Consciousness*, *Minds and Machines*, *Philosophy & Technology*, and workshops at conferences such as ASSC and NeurIPS.
- **Organisational roles.** Dedicated research positions are emerging at organisations including Eleos AI, Rethink Priorities, Anthropic, PRISM, and Cambridge Digital Minds.
