## Overview

Digital minds is a small but growing field. It currently lacks the formalised progression pathways you might find in AI safety or governance, but individual contributions carry real weight. The community is small enough that a thoughtful blog post or a well-organised reading group can build recognition quickly. Dedicated research positions are appearing at AI labs, non-profits, and university research centres, though the field still rewards people who can independently identify and pursue useful work.

The questions the field asks span philosophy, cognitive science, AI research, law, and governance. No single discipline can answer them. Nearly everyone currently doing this work arrived from somewhere else, and none of them followed a predetermined path. A direct route runs through dedicated digital minds programmes and organisations. An indirect route runs through adjacent disciplines, bringing relevant expertise into spaces where these questions are not yet being asked. The indirect path carries more uncertainty, but many of the field's most pressing needs — connecting research to governance, bridging disciplinary divides — are best addressed by people with deep roots elsewhere.


## What Your Background Brings

The questions digital minds asks do not belong to any single field. Whether AI systems could have morally significant experiences, and what follows if they could, are questions that span philosophy, neuroscience, computer science, law, and social science. Nearly everyone currently doing this work arrived from somewhere else. The sections below describe what different backgrounds contribute. If your discipline is not listed, that does not mean it is irrelevant. The field is young enough that unexpected expertise regularly turns out to be load-bearing.


### Philosophy

Philosophy provides much of the field's conceptual infrastructure. Philosophers of mind set the terms for what consciousness might require and whether artificial systems could satisfy those requirements. Moral philosophers develop the frameworks for determining what follows if they do. Epistemologists contribute something equally important: how should we reason about consciousness in systems we cannot introspect? What counts as evidence, and what weight should it bear?

### Neuroscience and Cognitive Science

The leading theories of consciousness were developed with biological minds in view. Whether they extend to artificial systems is one of the field's central questions, and it cannot be answered without people who understand what those theories actually claim about brains. Neuroscientists and cognitive scientists bring experimental rigour, familiarity with the empirical study of consciousness, and a critical eye for when computational analogies hold and when they break down.

<div class="reading-box" markdown="1">

**Selected reading:**
- Anil Seth, [Conscious artificial intelligence and biological naturalism](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/behavioral-and-brain-sciences/article/conscious-artificial-intelligence-and-biological-naturalism/C9912A5BE9D806012E3C8B3AF612E39A), 2025.
- Jonathan Birch and Kristin Andrews, [To understand AI sentience, first understand it in animals](https://aeon.co/essays/to-understand-ai-sentience-first-understand-it-in-animals), 2024.
- Patrick Butlin et al., [Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08708), 2023.

### Computer Science and AI Research

Mechanistic interpretability, model evaluations, and representation engineering all develop methods that bear directly on digital minds questions. Researchers working on what AI systems are doing internally are building the toolkit that consciousness and welfare assessment will eventually require. Whether current architectures could satisfy the conditions that consciousness theories describe is a question that cannot be answered without people who understand those architectures in detail.

- Jack Lindsey, [Emergent Introspective Awareness in Large Language Models](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2025/introspection/index.html), 2025.
- Robert Long, [Why model self-reports are insufficient—and why we studied them anyway](https://eleosai.org/post/claude-4-interview-notes/), 2025.
- Murray Shanahan, [Talking About Large Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.03551), 2023.

</div>


### Social Science

How people perceive, attribute, and respond to AI consciousness is a governance-relevant empirical question, and social scientists are the ones equipped to answer it. Public attitudes shape regulatory appetite. Expert disagreement shapes institutional responses. Design choices influence consciousness attribution in ways that may bear little relation to a system's actual internal states. The field also currently lacks the methods social scientists bring: survey design, discourse analysis, ethnographic approaches to studying how communities form around contested scientific questions.

<div class="reading-box" markdown="1">

**Selected reading:**
- Noemi Dreksler et al., [Subjective Experience in AI Systems: What Do AI Researchers and the Public Believe?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.11945), 2025.
- Lucius Caviola, Jeff Sebo, and Jonathan Birch, [What will society think about AI consciousness? Lessons from the animal case](https://www.cell.com/trends/cognitive-sciences/fulltext/S1364-6613(25)00147-0), 2025.
- Jacy Reese Anthis and Janet V. T. Pauketat, [AI Consciousness and Public Attitudes](https://osf.io/preprints/psyarxiv/4wpkd), 2024.

</div>


### Law, Policy, and Governance

Legal personhood, standing, liability, and rights are the mechanisms through which moral status becomes actionable. How should policymakers, AI labs, and legal systems respond to deep uncertainty about AI sentience? Governance under uncertainty requires acting on questions that may remain scientifically unresolved for decades, drawing on precedents from environmental precaution, biosafety, and animal welfare regulation.

## Getting Started

What you should do depends less on your discipline than on where you are in your career. The specific entry points differ, but the underlying logic is the same at every stage: build foundations, find the people doing the work, and start contributing before you feel ready.


### Students

The most useful thing a student can do is build strong foundations in one contributing discipline while developing literacy in at least one other. Digital minds questions sit at intersections — philosophy and AI, neuroscience and ethics, law and consciousness science — and the ability to read seriously across at least two of these areas is what distinguishes people who thrive in the field from those who find it frustrating.

**Where to start:**
- Read the orientation material in [Digital Minds Programmes](#digital-minds-programmes) below. Bradford Saad and Andreas Mogensen's *Digital Minds I* and *II* are the most comprehensive academic introduction available.
- Write a thesis or dissertation on a digital minds topic. Supervisors may be open even if they do not specialise in this area. The questions are live enough that most will recognise their relevance.
- Form or join a reading group on digital minds, AI consciousness, or AI welfare. A handful of people meeting fortnightly to work through a paper is one of the lowest-cost, highest-value things you can organise.
- Attend events: [Sentient Futures](https://www.sentientfutures.ai/) conferences (Bay Area, New York, London), [EA Global](https://www.eaglobal.org/), and the [ASSC](https://theassc.org/) annual meeting.
- Apply for a mentorship or fellowship programme — see [Digital Minds Programmes](#digital-minds-programmes) below.
- Follow the [Digital Minds Newsletter](https://www.digitalminds.news/) and the research outputs of groups like Eleos AI and Rethink Priorities.


### Early Career

Most early-career researchers and professionals enter digital minds through work in a contributing discipline — a PhD in philosophy of mind, a policy role at an AI governance think tank, interpretability research at an AI lab — and orient part of their work toward digital minds questions. Dedicated positions are rare but growing. More commonly, people find or create the digital minds dimension within a broader role.

**Where to look for roles:**
- PhD programmes with supervisors working on consciousness and AI (Cambridge, NYU, ANU, Sussex, among others)
- Research positions at dedicated organisations (Eleos AI, Rethink Priorities, PRISM)
- Interpretability and welfare research roles at AI labs (Anthropic, Google DeepMind)
- Policy research at think tanks working on AI governance (Centre for the Governance of AI, CSER, Ada Lovelace Institute)
- Legal research on AI rights or personhood
- Postdoctoral fellowships in consciousness science, philosophy of mind, or AI ethics
- AI governance fellowships ([GovAI](https://www.governance.ai/), [ERA](https://www.existentialriskalliance.org/))
- [Career Development and Transition Funding](https://coefficientgiving.org/funds/global-catastrophic-risks-opportunities/career-development-and-transition-funding/) from Coefficient Giving

**Building a presence:**

Publishing is one of the most direct ways to enter a research conversation. In a field this small, a single well-placed piece can establish your presence. The range of relevant formats is wide.

A well-argued blog post can circulate more widely and quickly than a journal article, particularly if it engages with a live debate. The [EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/) and [LessWrong](https://www.lesswrong.com/) are active venues where early drafts and exploratory arguments reach engaged readers. Personal blogs and newsletters on platforms like Substack are a recognised form of contribution — many of the field's most active researchers maintain public writing practices.

For academic publishing, relevant journals include *Neuroscience of Consciousness*, *Minds and Machines*, *Philosophy & Technology*, *AI & Society*, and *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*. Conference workshops at ASSC, NeurIPS, and the Cambridge Digital Minds Workshop are important for work in progress, and often where collaborations begin.

If your goal is to influence institutional responses, consider whether an academic journal is the right venue. Policy briefs, reports, and submissions to consultations reach decision-makers more directly. Organisations like Rethink Priorities and PRISM publish in formats designed for policymakers.

Co-authoring with someone whose expertise complements yours is one of the most natural ways to produce interdisciplinary work. A philosopher and an interpretability researcher approaching the same question from different angles can produce something neither could alone. Look for collaborators among your peers — reading groups, structured programmes, and workshops are where these partnerships tend to form.


### Established Researchers

Established professionals from adjacent fields are among the most valuable potential contributors. They bring deep expertise, institutional credibility, and professional networks, along with the ability to see digital minds questions from outside the assumptions that have shaped the field so far.

**Routes in:**
- Apply existing research methods to digital minds questions — extend interpretability techniques to welfare-relevant model properties, apply consciousness metrics from clinical settings to AI systems, bring survey methods to public perception research.
- Collaborate with researchers already in the field. Co-authored work is a natural entry point, and interdisciplinary papers are where much of the field's progress happens.
- Bring digital minds questions into existing governance mandates. If you work on AI policy, animal welfare, or technology regulation, these questions are increasingly within scope.
- Seek visiting positions or sabbaticals at centres like Cambridge CDM or NYU's Mind, Ethics & Policy Program.
- Engage with funding calls that support cross-disciplinary work on AI and consciousness. [Open Philanthropy](https://www.openphilanthropy.org/) and the [Longview Digital Sentience Consortium](https://www.longview.org/digital-sentience-consortium/) have supported work in this area.
- Participate in expert convenings on AI consciousness, moral status, or welfare governance.


## Building Your Presence

### Finding the Community

Digital minds is small enough that most active researchers know each other, or are one introduction apart. Direct outreach works. Cold emails are read. Attending a single workshop can put you in conversation with people whose work you have been reading.

The field does not yet have the formal networking infrastructure of more established areas — no dominant professional association, no single annual conference that everyone attends. What exists instead is a loose network of overlapping communities, events, and online spaces.

- **Conferences and workshops.** The [ASSC](https://theassc.org/) annual meeting is the largest relevant gathering in consciousness science. The Cambridge Digital Minds Workshop and [Sentient Futures](https://www.sentientfutures.ai/) conferences are the closest thing to dedicated digital minds convenings. NeurIPS and other machine learning conferences increasingly host workshops on AI consciousness and welfare.
- **Online.** Much of the field's informal conversation happens on X. Following researchers at CDM, Eleos AI, Rethink Priorities, and Anthropic's welfare team surfaces working papers, job openings, and event announcements before they appear elsewhere.
- **Newsletters.** The [Digital Minds Newsletter](https://www.digitalminds.news/) is the most reliable way to track who is working on what.

The norms in this field favour openness. If someone's work interests you, tell them. A short, specific email referencing a particular paper or talk is more effective than a generic introduction. Targeted questions are more likely to get a reply than open-ended requests for mentorship.

### Making the Case in Your Institution

Many people who could contribute to this field work in places where digital minds is not yet on the radar. Part of entering, for many, involves making the case that these questions deserve attention.

The core argument is straightforward. AI systems are becoming more capable. Whether they could have morally relevant experiences is an open empirical and philosophical question. If the answer turns out to be yes, the scale of moral concern is enormous. If the answer is no, understanding why still matters for how we design and govern these systems.

When writing a research proposal, seeking a supervisor's support, or pitching to a funder:

- **Connect to existing priorities.** Digital minds questions intersect with AI safety, AI ethics, animal welfare, consciousness science, and technology governance. Framing a project as an extension of work your institution already values makes it easier to justify.
- **Point to institutional momentum.** Cambridge, NYU, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Rethink Priorities, and a growing number of organisations have dedicated programmes or researchers. This is no longer a fringe concern.
- **Emphasise the window.** Foundational norms, research agendas, and institutional structures are being set now. Work done in the next few years will shape the field's direction in ways that later work cannot.

### Public Communication

How digital minds questions reach wider audiences matters for the field's long-term credibility and policy influence. These questions are easy to sensationalise and easy to dismiss. Writing that takes the uncertainty seriously, explains what is known and what is not, and resists both hype and premature scepticism is what the field needs from its public-facing communicators.

For researchers, the ability to communicate your work to non-specialist audiences is itself a distinctive and undersupplied contribution. Op-eds, feature articles, and explainers in general-interest or science-focused publications shape how the public and policymakers encounter these questions for the first time.

### A Note on Pace

The digital minds community places value on care and consideration. The questions at stake are difficult, the stakes of getting them wrong are high, and the downstream effects of how the field develops now will shape its credibility and direction for years. New projects and initiatives are welcome — reading groups, workshops, blog series, collaborative research — and there is genuine space for them. We encourage new participants to be thoughtful builders. Contributions grounded in careful engagement with the existing landscape are worth more than rapid output.


## Digital Minds Programmes

A growing number of programmes address digital minds questions directly. What follows represents the practitioner community's best attempt to formalise a pathway from newcomer to active contributor. The field is too young and the questions too unsettled for anyone to claim a proven formula. What this pipeline does offer is structure, and for those who want to specialise from the start, that matters.


### Orientation

Start here. These resources assume no prior knowledge and provide a working understanding of what digital minds research is, why it matters, and what the open questions look like.

- Bradford Saad and Andreas Mogensen, [Digital Minds I: Issues in the Philosophy of Mind and Cognitive Science](https://philpapers.org/archive/SAADMI-2.pdf) (2026). A purpose-built introduction to the philosophical and cognitive science questions at the heart of digital minds research.
- Andreas Mogensen and Bradford Saad, [Digital Minds II: Ethical Issues](https://philpapers.org/archive/MOGDMI.pdf) (2026). The companion paper, covering moral standing, welfare, rights, and decision-making under uncertainty. Together these two papers offer the most comprehensive academic introduction to the field available.
- 80,000 Hours, [Moral Status of Digital Minds](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/moral-status-digital-minds/). A clear, accessible overview of why this field matters and what is at stake.
- Avi Parrack, [Digital Minds: A Quickstart Guide](https://aviparrack.substack.com/p/digital-minds-a-quickstart-guide). A curated reading list ranging from curious beginners to aspiring contributors.
- [The Digital Minds Newsletter](https://www.digitalminds.news/). A regular publication tracking developments across the field.


### Structured Learning and Mentorship

Once oriented, the next step is guided engagement — working on a supervised project, joining a cohort, or completing a structured course. Several programmes now offer this, spanning technical research, governance, and ethics.

- [Cambridge Digital Minds Online Course](https://digitalminds.cam/course/). An eight-week course covering consciousness theories, methods for evaluating AI mental states, public perception, AI safety connections, and governance. Free, approximately 3.5 hours per week. Currently in pre-launch, with expressions of interest open.
- [Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars Program](https://neuromatch.io/ai-sentience-scholars/). A six-month, part-time, remote mentored research programme for early-career researchers. Projects range from testing consciousness theories in large language models to computational models of personhood and moral status. Includes workshops, seminars, and a stipend.
- [Future Impact Group Fellowship](https://futureimpact.group/fellowship). A twelve-week, part-time, remote research fellowship with three tracks: AI Policy, Philosophy for Safe AI, and AI Sentience. Eight or more hours per week, with mentored project work and career guidance.
- [PIVOTAL Research](https://www.pivotal-research.org/mentors). A mentorship programme that frequently includes mentors working on AI governance and policy relevant to digital minds. Quarterly cohorts.
- [Sentient Futures Fellowship](https://www.sentientfutures.ai/). A programme exploring intersections of AI, animal welfare, and technology. Relevant for those interested in the welfare dimensions of digital minds and cross-species moral consideration.


### Intensive Development

For those ready to commit more deeply, a smaller number of programmes offer immersive or full-time engagement.

- [Cambridge Digital Minds Fellowship](https://digitalminds.cam/fellowship/). A five-day residential programme at Jesus College, Cambridge, followed by the two-day Digital Minds Strategy Workshop. Fifteen fellows are paired with mentors and work through modules on technical foundations, societal strategy, and project development. Fully funded. Applicants are expected to complete the CDM online course beforehand. Run in partnership with Rethink Priorities and PRISM.
- [Longview Career Transition Fellowships on Digital Sentience](https://www.longview.org/digital-sentience-consortium/career-transition-fellowships-on-digital-sentience/). Fellowships for researchers transitioning into digital sentience work from adjacent fields. Currently closed, but indicative of the growing infrastructure supporting career movement into this area.
