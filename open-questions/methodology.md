## What this list is

This directory presents open research questions in digital minds, drawn from published research agendas, position papers, and monographs by researchers and organisations active in the field. Each question is traceable to a specific source passage. The aim is to give newcomers a map of what the field considers unsolved, and to signal where contributions from adjacent disciplines would be valuable.

## How the sources were selected

We identified 10 sources through three channels:

1. **Published research agendas.** Documents explicitly structured as agendas or lists of open questions. These formed the core of the collection: Anthis (2023), Shiller et al. (2024), Finnveden (2024), Caviola (2024), and the GPI Philosophy and Psychology agendas (both 2024).
2. **Position papers.** Documents from leading researchers or organisations that embed research questions within argumentative text, rather than listing them directly. Mogensen, Saad & Butlin (2024) and Eleos AI (2024) fall into this category.
3. **Monographs.** Goldstein & Kirk-Giannini's *AI Wellbeing* (2026), the first book-length treatment of the topic, was read selectively for its open-question content.
4. **Cross-references.** Sources cited within already-identified documents. Caviola (2024), for instance, was identified through the GPI Psychology agenda.

A source was included if it contained identifiable open research questions about digital minds, AI consciousness, AI welfare, AI moral status, or the societal implications of these topics. Sources focused purely on AI safety, alignment, or capabilities without a digital minds dimension were excluded.

### Coverage and limitations

The 10 sources span several disciplinary registers: philosophical foundations (Shiller et al., Mogensen et al., GPI Philosophy, Goldstein & Kirk-Giannini), empirical and technical research (Long, Eleos AI), policy and action (Finnveden, Anthis), and social science (Caviola, GPI Psychology).

Some limitations are worth noting. The sources were drawn largely from individuals and organisations currently working within digital minds research. This makes the list a narrow and provisional sample of the questions that are likely relevant. It reflects what the field's active participants consider important, which is valuable, but it also risks reproducing shared assumptions. Future versions of this list will draw from a broader range of epistemic cultures and disciplinary perspectives.

The list also reflects the field as of early 2026. Digital minds research is moving quickly, and new questions will emerge as the empirical and institutional landscape shifts.

## How the questions were extracted

Each source was read in full. For longer works (the GPI Philosophy Agenda and the Goldstein & Kirk-Giannini monograph), relevant chapters were identified through the table of contents and read selectively.

For each source, every identifiable research question was recorded alongside its page number and the passage from which it was drawn. Questions fell into two main categories:

- **Quoted.** The source phrases the question explicitly. We reproduce it verbatim.
- **Derived.** The source discusses a research direction or open problem in running prose without formulating a discrete question. We drafted a question that captures the concern, and cite the originating passage so readers can verify the interpretation.

Some sources also yielded questions that sit outside these two categories. Shiller et al. (2024) contains what we call **meta-questions**: template questions that apply across multiple traits (consciousness, sentience, agency, etc.), each generating several specific questions depending on which trait is substituted. Finnveden (2024) is structured around concrete **project ideas** rather than abstract research questions. Where a project proposal contained or implied a research question, we extracted it; proposals that are purely actionable were recorded separately.

This process yielded approximately 247 raw items across the 10 sources.

## How the list was consolidated

The raw extraction produced around 247 items, many of which overlap substantially across sources. The consolidation step merged duplicates, synthesised questions that approach the same problem from different angles, and grouped related questions by domain. Where multiple sources raise the same question, all are cited. The full per-source extraction files, with page references and originating passages, are available for anyone who wants to trace a question back to its source.
