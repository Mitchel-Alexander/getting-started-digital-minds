### An afternoon

- Read the [80,000 Hours profile on the moral status of digital minds](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/moral-status-digital-minds/). The clearest short overview of what is at stake and why the field exists.
- Subscribe to the [Digital Minds Newsletter](https://www.digitalminds.news/). A regular digest tracking developments across the field.
- Browse [Avi Parrack's Quickstart Guide](https://aviparrack.substack.com/p/digital-minds-a-quickstart-guide). A curated reading list organised by level of engagement, from curious to committed.
- Watch an episode of [Exploring Machine Consciousness](https://www.youtube.com/@PRISM_Global) (PRISM), [Our Lives With Bots](https://www.youtube.com/@OurLivesWithBots), or [Conspicuous Cognition](https://www.youtube.com/@conspicuouscognition/videos). Three channels covering consciousness, AI, and moral status from different angles.

### A weekend

- Read Bradford Saad and Andreas Mogensen's [Digital Minds I](https://philpapers.org/archive/SAADMI-2.pdf) (2026). The most comprehensive academic introduction to the philosophical and cognitive science questions at the centre of the field.
- Read Patrick Butlin et al., [Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08708) (2023). The field's anchor paper, applying multiple consciousness theories to AI architectures.
- Read one sceptical or alternative perspective. Anil Seth's [Conscious Artificial Intelligence and Biological Naturalism](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/behavioral-and-brain-sciences/article/conscious-artificial-intelligence-and-biological-naturalism/C9912A5BE9D806012E3C8B3AF612E39A) (2025) argues consciousness may require biological substrates. John Dorsch's [Against AI Welfare](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aaai.70016) (2025) challenges the coherence of the concept. Either will sharpen your sense of where the disagreements lie.
- Write something. A short summary, a reaction post, a set of questions the reading raised. Even a few hundred words on a personal blog or shared document begins to clarify your own thinking and signals engagement to others in the field.

### Going deeper

- Start or join a reading group. If your department or institution does not have one, propose it. A handful of people meeting fortnightly to work through a paper is one of the lowest-cost, highest-value things you can organise.
- Register for the [Cambridge Digital Minds online course](https://digitalminds.cam/course/). Eight weeks covering consciousness theories, welfare assessment, governance, and public perception. Free, approximately 3.5 hours per week. Currently in pre-launch.
- Apply for a structured programme. The [Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars Program](https://neuromatch.io/ai-sentience-scholars/), [Future Impact Group Fellowship](https://futureimpact.group/fellowship), and [Sentient Futures Fellowship](https://www.sentientfutures.ai/) all offer mentored research with cohorts of peers. See [Pathways](/pathways/) for the full pipeline.
- Attend a conference or workshop. The [Association for the Scientific Study of Consciousness](https://theassc.org/) (ASSC), the Cambridge Digital Minds Workshop, and [Sentient Futures conferences](https://www.youtube.com/@sentfutures/videos) are the main venues where this community gathers.
