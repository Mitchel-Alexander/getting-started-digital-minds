### An afternoon

- Read the [80,000 Hours profile on the moral status of digital minds](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/moral-status-digital-minds/). The best short introduction to the field: what the questions are, why they matter, and where the uncertainty lies.
- Subscribe to the [Digital Minds Newsletter](https://www.digitalminds.news/). The closest thing the field has to a shared bulletin board. Covers new research, events, job openings, and policy developments as they happen.
- Browse [Avi Parrack's Quickstart Guide](https://aviparrack.substack.com/p/digital-minds-a-quickstart-guide). A curated reading list that scales with your level of commitment, from a single paper to a full syllabus.
- Watch an episode of [Exploring Machine Consciousness](https://www.youtube.com/@PRISM_Global) (PRISM), [Our Lives With Bots](https://www.youtube.com/@OurLivesWithBots), or [Conspicuous Cognition](https://www.youtube.com/@conspicuouscognition/videos). Three channels approaching consciousness, AI, and moral status from different directions. Good for getting a feel for how people in the field actually talk about these questions.

### A weekend

- Read Bradford Saad and Andreas Mogensen's [Digital Minds I](https://philpapers.org/archive/SAADMI-2.pdf) (2026). The most comprehensive academic introduction available. It covers the philosophical and cognitive science questions at the centre of the field, and it does so without assuming prior expertise in either.
- Read Patrick Butlin et al., [Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08708) (2023). The paper that catalysed the field's current form. Fourteen researchers applied leading consciousness theories to AI architectures and found that none could be confidently ruled in or out. Most work since has been responding to it.
- Read one sceptical voice. Anil Seth's [Conscious Artificial Intelligence and Biological Naturalism](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/behavioral-and-brain-sciences/article/conscious-artificial-intelligence-and-biological-naturalism/C9912A5BE9D806012E3C8B3AF612E39A) (2025) argues consciousness may require biological substrates. John Dorsch's [Against AI Welfare](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/aaai.70016) (2025) challenges the coherence of the welfare concept itself. The field takes both positions seriously. You should too.
- Write something. A summary, a reaction, a set of questions the reading raised. A few hundred words on a blog or shared document forces you to work out what you actually think, and it signals to others in the field that you are engaging.

### Going deeper

- Start or join a reading group. If your department or institution does not have one, propose it. A handful of people meeting fortnightly to work through a paper is one of the lowest-cost, highest-value things you can organise. Several of the field's current collaborations began this way.
- Register for the [Cambridge Digital Minds online course](https://digitalminds.cam/course/). Eight weeks covering consciousness theories, welfare assessment, governance, and public perception. Free, approximately 3.5 hours per week. Currently in pre-launch.
- Apply for a structured programme. The [Neuromatch AI Sentience Scholars Program](https://neuromatch.io/ai-sentience-scholars/), [Future Impact Group Fellowship](https://futureimpact.group/fellowship), and [Sentient Futures Fellowship](https://www.sentientfutures.ai/) all offer mentored research within a cohort. See [Pathways](/pathways/) for the full pipeline and how these programmes connect.
- Attend a conference or workshop. The [Association for the Scientific Study of Consciousness](https://theassc.org/) (ASSC) annual meeting is the largest relevant gathering. The Cambridge Digital Minds Workshop and [Sentient Futures conferences](https://www.youtube.com/@sentfutures/videos) are where the dedicated digital minds community convenes. Showing up to one of these puts you in the room with people whose work you have been reading.
