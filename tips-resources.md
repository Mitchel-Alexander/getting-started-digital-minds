The earlier sections of this guide describe the field and how to enter it. This one is practical. It covers what to do first, how to present yourself, where to find people, and how to build the skills and visibility that make a contribution possible.

Some of this advice applies broadly. Some is specific to digital minds. Where the field's youth or interdisciplinary character creates distinctive challenges, we say so.


## Building Connections

Digital minds is small enough that most active researchers know each other, or are one introduction apart. This has practical consequences for newcomers. Direct outreach works. Cold emails are read. Attending a single workshop can put you in conversation with people whose work you have been reading.

The field does not yet have the formal networking infrastructure of more established areas. There is no equivalent of the annual conference that everyone attends, no dominant professional association, no single mailing list. What exists instead is a loose network of overlapping communities, events, and online spaces.

### Where to find people

- **Conferences and workshops.** The [Association for the Scientific Study of Consciousness](https://theassc.org/) (ASSC) annual meeting is the largest relevant gathering in consciousness science. The Cambridge Digital Minds Workshop and [Sentient Futures conferences](https://www.youtube.com/@sentfutures/videos) (Bay Area, New York, London) are the closest thing to dedicated digital minds convenings. NeurIPS and other machine learning conferences increasingly host workshops on AI consciousness and welfare.
- **Social media.** Much of the field's informal conversation happens on X (formerly Twitter). Following researchers at CDM, Eleos AI, Rethink Priorities, and Anthropic's welfare team is a reasonable starting point. Threads and replies often surface working papers, job openings, and event announcements before they appear elsewhere.
- **Newsletters.** The [Digital Minds Newsletter](https://www.digitalminds.news/) and the outputs of organisations listed in [Research Areas](/research-areas/) are the most reliable way to track who is working on what.

### Reaching out

The norms in this field favour openness. If someone's work interests you, tell them. A short, specific email referencing a particular paper or talk is more effective than a generic introduction. Researchers in digital minds are generally responsive, though the most prominent figures receive more correspondence than they can answer. Targeted questions are more likely to get a reply than open-ended requests for mentorship.

### Building together

Some of the most useful work in a young field comes from small groups of motivated people organising themselves. Reading groups, workshops, blog series, or collaborative research projects among peers can produce real contributions. The field benefits from thoughtful new initiatives, and there is genuine space for them.

A note on pace. The digital minds community places value on care and consideration. The questions at stake are difficult, the stakes of getting them wrong are high, and the downstream effects of how the field develops now will shape its credibility and direction for years. New projects are welcome, and the field has genuine space for them. We encourage new participants to be thoughtful builders. The questions this community works on carry real moral weight, and contributions that reflect that, ones grounded in careful engagement with the existing landscape, are worth more than rapid output.


## Making the Case for Digital Minds

Many people who could contribute to this field work in institutions where digital minds is not yet on the radar. Departments, funding bodies, and employers may not recognise it as a legitimate area of research or policy. Part of entering the field, for many, involves making the case that it deserves attention.

The core argument is straightforward. AI systems are becoming more capable. Whether they could have morally relevant experiences is an open empirical and philosophical question. If the answer turns out to be yes, the scale of moral concern is enormous. If the answer is no, understanding why still matters for how we design and govern these systems. Either way, the question warrants serious investigation, and most disciplines have something distinctive to offer.

### What your discipline brings

Digital minds is shaped by the perspectives people bring into it. The field's interdisciplinary character is a structural feature, not a temporary phase. The questions it asks cannot be answered from within any single tradition. Below are some of the gaps that specific disciplines are well positioned to fill.

- **Philosophers** bring the conceptual precision the field depends on. What counts as consciousness? What grounds moral status? When is uncertainty sufficient to warrant precaution?
- **Neuroscientists and cognitive scientists** bring empirical grounding. The leading theories of consciousness were developed for biological minds. Whether they extend to artificial systems requires people who understand what those theories actually claim.
- **Computer scientists and AI researchers** bring knowledge of what these systems actually do. Interpretability research and model evaluations are building the toolkit that consciousness and welfare assessment will require.
- **Social scientists** bring methods for studying how people think about, attribute, and respond to AI consciousness. Public attitudes shape policy. Expert disagreement shapes institutional responses.
- **Lawyers** bring frameworks for translating moral and philosophical conclusions into institutional form. Legal personhood, standing, liability, and rights are the mechanisms through which moral status becomes actionable.
- **Journalists and public communicators** bring the ability to make these questions legible to audiences encountering them for the first time. How digital minds questions are framed in public shapes whether they are taken seriously.

### Pitching digital minds work

If you are writing a research proposal, seeking a supervisor's support, or making the case to a funder, a few framing strategies tend to work.

**Connect to existing priorities.** Digital minds questions intersect with AI safety, AI ethics, animal welfare, consciousness science, and technology governance. Framing a digital minds project as an extension of work your institution already values makes it easier to justify.

**Point to institutional momentum.** Cambridge, NYU, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Rethink Priorities, and a growing number of organisations have dedicated programmes or researchers. This is no longer a fringe concern.

**Emphasise the window.** Foundational norms, research agendas, and institutional structures are being set now. Work done in the next few years will shape the field's direction in ways that later work cannot.


## Skills for Digital Minds Work

There is no standard skill set for digital minds. The field draws on too many disciplines for any single profile to cover the ground. What follows are capacities that come up repeatedly as valuable, whether you are doing technical research, governance work, or something in between.

### Reading across disciplines

The single most transferable skill is the ability to engage seriously with work outside your training. A philosopher who can read an interpretability paper with comprehension. A computer scientist who can follow an argument about moral status without dismissing the methodology. A policy researcher who understands enough consciousness science to evaluate the claims being made. You do not need to become an expert in every contributing field. You need to be able to identify when another discipline's findings matter for your own questions, and to engage with those findings on their own terms.

### Communicating across boundaries

Related but distinct. Reading across disciplines is a private skill. Communicating across them is a public one. Much of the work in digital minds involves translating ideas between communities whose assumptions, methods, and vocabularies differ substantially. This includes writing for non-specialist audiences, but it also includes the harder task of making your own discipline's insights legible to researchers in adjacent fields without losing the substance.

### Comfort with genuine uncertainty

Digital minds is defined by questions that may remain unresolved for decades. Whether AI systems can be conscious, what moral status requires, how to govern under persistent ignorance. People who need clear answers before they can act productively will find this field frustrating. People who can hold open questions open, reason carefully about what follows from uncertainty itself, and make progress without premature resolution tend to thrive.

### What transfers from adjacent fields

Most of what you need, you already have. The specific transfers depend on where you are coming from.

- From **consciousness science and neuroscience**: experimental design, familiarity with the major theories, understanding of what evidence for consciousness actually looks like in biological systems.
- From **philosophy**: argument construction, conceptual analysis, the ability to make fine-grained distinctions that turn out to be load-bearing. Ethics training transfers directly to moral status questions.
- From **AI and machine learning research**: understanding of model architectures, training dynamics, and failure modes. Interpretability skills are directly applicable. Experience with evaluation design is increasingly relevant.
- From **animal welfare and ethics**: precedent for extending moral consideration under uncertainty, familiarity with precautionary frameworks, understanding of how welfare science interacts with policy.
- From **policy and governance**: institutional knowledge, stakeholder engagement, the ability to move from abstract principles to implementable frameworks. Experience with governance under scientific uncertainty is especially relevant.
- From **journalism and communications**: audience awareness, clarity under constraint, the ability to make a complex and contested topic accessible without flattening it.


## Publishing

Publishing is one of the most direct ways to enter a research conversation, and in a field this small, a single well-placed piece can establish your presence. The range of relevant formats is wide. The right choice depends on your audience, your career stage, and what you are trying to accomplish.

### Blog posts and online discussion

The fastest way to start publishing is to write about what you are reading and thinking. A well-argued blog post can circulate more widely and more quickly than a journal article, particularly if it engages with a live debate or responds to a recent development.

The [EA Forum](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/) is an active community of engaged and thoughtful individuals, many of whom work on or adjacent to digital minds questions. Publishing early drafts, research summaries, or exploratory arguments here is a good way to refine your thinking in public. The community is responsive, and the feedback you receive can sharpen work intended for publication elsewhere. [LessWrong](https://www.lesswrong.com/) hosts similar discussion, with a more technical and epistemics-focused readership.

Personal blogs and newsletters on platforms like Substack give you editorial control and the ability to build a direct audience. In a field where many of the most active researchers maintain public writing practices, this is a legitimate and recognised form of contribution. The trade-off is that quality control rests entirely with you. Seeking feedback from peers before publishing is worth the effort.

### Journalism and public communication

How digital minds questions reach wider audiences matters for the field's long-term credibility and policy influence. Writing for non-specialist readers, whether as a journalist covering the field or as a researcher communicating your own work, is a distinctive and undersupplied contribution.

Op-eds, feature articles, and explainers in general-interest or science-focused publications can shape how the public and policymakers encounter these questions for the first time. If you have the skills and inclination, pitching to outlets that cover AI, technology, or science is worth pursuing. The [Tarbell Fellowship](https://tarbellfellowship.org/) provides structured support for early-career writers focused on AI journalism.

Clarity matters here more than anywhere else. The questions digital minds raises are easy to sensationalise and easy to dismiss. Writing that takes the uncertainty seriously, explains what is known and what is not, and resists both hype and premature scepticism is what the field needs from its public-facing communicators.

### Policy writing

If your goal is to influence how institutions respond to digital minds questions, consider whether an academic journal is the right venue. Policy briefs, reports, and submissions to consultations and regulatory processes reach decision-makers more directly.

Organisations like Rethink Priorities and PRISM publish research in formats designed for policymakers. Contributing to these efforts, or producing your own governance-relevant writing, is a concrete way to connect research to institutional action. The gap between digital minds research and digital minds policy remains wide, and people who can work in that space are needed.

### Academic publishing

The field does not yet have a dedicated journal. Work appears across several disciplines' venues, which reflects the field's structure but can make it harder to know where to submit.

Journals that regularly publish digital minds research include *Neuroscience of Consciousness*, *Minds and Machines*, *Philosophy & Technology*, *AI & Society*, and *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*. Ethics-focused work appears in *Journal of Applied Philosophy*, *Ethics and Information Technology*, and the *Journal of AI Research*. Legal scholarship on AI personhood and rights appears in law reviews and interdisciplinary journals like *Law, Innovation and Technology*.

Conference workshops are an important venue, particularly for work in progress. The Association for the Scientific Study of Consciousness (ASSC), NeurIPS, and the Cambridge Digital Minds Workshop all host sessions where emerging research is presented and discussed. These settings are often where collaborations begin.

### Co-authoring

Writing with someone whose expertise complements yours is one of the most natural ways to produce interdisciplinary work, and digital minds rewards it. A philosopher and an interpretability researcher approaching the same question from different angles can produce something neither could alone. Co-authorship also builds relationships and, for newcomers, provides a way into a research conversation with the support of someone who knows the landscape.

Look for collaborators among your peers and in adjacent fields. Reading groups, structured programmes, and workshops are where these partnerships tend to form.
